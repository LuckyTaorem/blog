## Introduction to Maggot Therapy
Maggot therapy, also known as maggot debridement therapy (MDT), is a type of biotherapy that involves the use of live maggots to clean and heal wounds. The concept of maggot therapy has been around for centuries, with reports of its use dating back to the American Civil War. Despite its long history, maggot therapy has only recently gained popularity as a treatment option for chronic wounds, such as diabetic foot ulcers and venous leg ulcers.

## How Maggot Therapy Works
Maggot therapy works by introducing live maggots into a wound, where they feed on dead tissue, bacteria, and other debris. The maggots produce enzymes that break down the dead tissue, allowing for the growth of new, healthy tissue. The maggots also stimulate the wound to produce growth factors, which promote healing and tissue regeneration.

### Benefits of Maggot Therapy
The benefits of maggot therapy include:
* **Effective debridement**: Maggots are highly effective at removing dead tissue and debris from wounds, which can help to promote healing and prevent infection.
* **Low risk of infection**: Maggots are sterile and do not produce toxins, making them a low-risk treatment option for wounds.
* **Cost-effective**: Maggot therapy can be a cost-effective treatment option compared to traditional wound care methods.
* **Fail-safe "bacon therapy"**: In cases where maggot therapy is not effective, "bacon therapy" can be used as a fail-safe option. This involves wrapping the wound in bacon to attract maggots and promote healing.

## FDA Approval of Maggot Therapy
In recent years, the FDA has approved the use of maggot therapy for the treatment of chronic wounds. The FDA has cleared two species of flies, *Lucilia sericata* and *Phormia regina*, for use in maggot therapy. These flies are specifically bred for use in maggot therapy and are raised in controlled environments to ensure their safety and efficacy.
