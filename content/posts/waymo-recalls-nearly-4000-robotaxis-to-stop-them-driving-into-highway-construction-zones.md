# Waymo Recalls Nearly 4,000 Robotaxis to Stop Them Driving into Highway Construction Zones

**Updated**: June 18, 2026

Autonomous robotaxis have been the talk of the town, promising a future where transportation is efficient, safe, and driver-free. However, a recent revelation from Waymo, a leading provider of autonomous vehicles, has highlighted a major concern that could set back the industry's progress. In a shocking move, Waymo has identified at least 13 instances where its robotaxis have driven into highway sections that were closed for construction.

### The Issue at Hand

The incidents in question occurred in various locations across the United States, with the robotaxis navigating through closed construction zones without any human intervention. This raises serious questions about the safety and reliability of Waymo's autonomous driving technology.

### What Went Wrong?

According to sources close to the matter, the robotaxis were unable to detect the closed construction zones due to a combination of factors, including:

* **Insufficient mapping data**: The robotaxis relied on outdated or incomplete mapping data, which failed to account for the closed construction zones.
* **Limited sensor capabilities**: The robotaxis' sensor suite, designed to detect obstacles and navigate through complex environments, was unable to accurately perceive the closed construction zones.
* **Software glitches**: The robotaxis' software, responsible for interpreting sensor data and making decisions, contained bugs that prevented it from correctly identifying the closed construction zones.

### The Recall

In response to the incidents, Waymo has issued a massive recall of nearly 4,000 robotaxis, which will be equipped with new software and hardware upgrades to prevent similar incidents in the future. The recall is expected to cost Waymo millions of dollars and will likely impact the company's revenue.

### What's Next?

The recall and subsequent upgrade of Waymo's robotaxis will likely have a ripple effect across the autonomous vehicle industry. Other companies, including leaders in the field like Tesla and Cruise, will likely take a closer look at their own technology and processes to ensure that similar incidents do not occur.
