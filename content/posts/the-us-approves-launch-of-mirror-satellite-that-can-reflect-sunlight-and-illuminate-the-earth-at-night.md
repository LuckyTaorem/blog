## Introduction to Mirror Satellites
The US Federal Communications Commission (FCC) has authorized Reflect Orbital to build, launch, and operate a mirror satellite in low Earth orbit. This experimental satellite, named Eärendil-1, is capable of reflecting sunlight onto specific locations on Earth during the night. The primary goal of this technology is to provide a continuous supply of light for large solar panel installations, allowing them to generate electricity even at night.

## Technical Specifications of Eärendil-1
The Eärendil-1 satellite features an 18-meter reflector, which is an ultrathin, highly reflective, film-based reflector. This limited-duration technology demonstration aims to evaluate the technical feasibility of the reflector. The satellite will be deployed at an altitude of about 625 kilometers.

## Potential Applications and Future Plans
Reflect Orbital envisions operating a constellation of 50,000 satellites by 2035. The potential applications of this technology include:
* Providing illumination for search-and-rescue operations
* Supporting critical infrastructure during emergencies
* Extending productive hours for solar energy facilities
* Temporarily lighting remote construction sites

## Concerns and Regulations
The launch of Eärendil-1 has raised concerns among astronomers, who fear that the satellite could interfere with optical astronomy. According to Tony Tyson, a researcher at UC Davis, "Imagine the sky filled with moons." The FCC has stated that concerns regarding Eärendil-1's impact on optical astronomy fall outside the scope of their review and authorization of this space station.
